This page exists to explain why we built a skincare brand with 4 products — and why we think that's not a limitation. It's the whole point.

part one
Nobody actually needs 12 products.

The average skincare routine recommended by a "skincare influencer" in 2024 has 8 to 14 steps. Double cleanse, toner, essence, vitamin C serum, eye cream, moisturiser, SPF — and that's just the morning. By the time you've done all of it, it's noon.

Here's what's actually happening: each of those products exists because a brand made it, marketed it, and convinced you it was non-negotiable. Some of them are useful. Most of them are redundant when the products around them are formulated correctly.

Nobody is selling you a 2-step routine because a 2-step routine doesn't have a 14-product cart value. The complexity is, at least partly, a business model.

"The skincare industry generates roughly $180 billion globally. A significant portion of that comes from selling people additional steps they didn't know they needed until someone told them they did."

We're not saying every extra product is useless. We're saying your baseline — the routine that keeps your skin healthy, protected, and not breaking down — does not require 12 of them. It requires 4.

part two
The products we eliminated and why.

Before we landed on 4, we mapped every product category in a standard routine and asked one question: is this doing something that our 4 products aren't already covering?

Separate toner our cleanser maintains skin pH — toner's main job, done.
Vitamin C serum effective, but unstable. niacinamide covers brightness without the drama.
Standalone eye cream the eye area is just thin skin. night cream with peptides works.
Exfoliating scrub BHA in the face wash does it chemically — gentler, more consistent.
Separate day moisturiser our sunscreen has HA and niacinamide. it's already moisturising.
Sheet masks, mists, essences we couldn't make a case for these with a straight face.

What was left after we cut everything redundant:

Face Wash cleanse + active treatment + barrier care
Body Wash because your body has skin and deserves the same logic
Sunscreen non-negotiable. the one thing dermatologists fully agree on.
Night Cream repair, retinol, pigmentation — while you sleep
part three
Multifunctional isn't a shortcut. It's harder to make.

The easy version of this brand would be four mediocre products with good packaging and a minimalist mission statement. We know. We've seen those brands.

The hard version — which is what this is — is formulating each product to do the work of two or three without destabilising the actives, irritating the skin, or compromising on what any individual ingredient can achieve.

0
The number of formulation iterations the face wash went through before the niacinamide, salicylic acid, and ceramide combination was stable enough, gentle enough, and effective enough to ship. Multifunctional products aren't simpler to make. They're significantly more complex. The simplicity is entirely on your end.

Every active in every product is at a concentration that does something measurable. Not decorative. Not "trace amounts so we can put it on the label." The niacinamide in the face wash is at 5% — the evidence-backed range. The retinol in the night cream is at a dose that causes real cell turnover. The SPF is 50 PA++++, not 30 PA++.

If you know what any of that means: you're welcome. If you don't: your skin still gets the benefit. You just don't have to care about the numbers.

The reason the products are colour-coded the way they are — slate, off-white, sand, black — is that we wanted each one to feel like its own thing. Not a matching set you display on a shelf for the aesthetic. Four distinct objects with four distinct jobs that happen to work together.

A group project where everyone actually pulls their weight. You know how rare that is.

Why we'll probably
never make a 5th product.

People ask us this a lot. Usually with some version of "but what about [ingredient]?" or "you're missing [step]."

The honest answer is that every time we've had the internal debate about a 5th product, we've ended up asking the same question: are we making this because it genuinely belongs here, or because we want more revenue and need a reason to justify it?

So far, the answer has been the second one. So we haven't made it.

If that ever changes — if we find something that genuinely doesn't belong in any of the 4 and can't be reformulated in — we'll make it and we'll tell you exactly why. That's the deal. Until then: 4 products. That's the whole company.

The constraint isn't a marketing angle. It's what happens when you keep asking "does this actually need to exist?" until you can't cut any further.
